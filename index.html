The phrase "differential equation" is intimidating. It sounds like a fake science word from a movie, like 'tachyon pulse' or 'flux capacitor.' But DE's are also among the best tools we have to design systems and make sense of nature. This piece explains

  1. what makes differential equations different from a regular equations
  2. how to solve a simple class of them.

It should be readable if you've taken calc.

Derivative refresher

The derivative of a function is basically its slope. That slope can be rendered as the height of a triangle whose base is $1$ and whose hypoteneuse is tangent to $y$ at $t$. (This makes more sense when you try it out!) Below, hover over the left plot to see $y$'s derivatives, which are plotted on the right as the derivative function $y'(t)$.

What makes DE's different?

Maybe you're used to solving equations like $$ t^2 - 2t = 1.$$ Solving this equation means finding a value of $t$ that makes the left- and right-hand sides equal. The solution is a number.

A differential equation, by contrast, is statement like $$ y'(t) + \lambda \cdot y(t) = 0$$ that involves the derivative of an unknown function $y(t)$. So we start out knowing some fact about $y$'s derivative, and we have figure out what $y$ is.

And you shall derive our velocity

Now it's time to gaze upon the man-melting power of the mother of all DE's:$$ y'(t) = .5 \cdot y(t)$$. Just try to integrate this beast away. You can't because $y(t)$ is already in the equation, crouched and waiting for you with a sharp knife.

You'll solve this DE visually, but this time the horizontal axis of the right plot isn't $t$, it's $y$ itself. That's because the equation says, "The derivative of $y$ is always one-half of $y$ itself." So if you draw $y(t)$ correctly, its derivative will always fall on the straight line $y' = 0.5 \cdot y$.
